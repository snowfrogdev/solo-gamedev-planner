# Jakob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other sites. This means that users prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.

## Description

Jakob's Law captures the fundamental truth that users build expectations about how interfaces should work based on their cumulative experience across all the other products and websites they use. When your interface follows established conventions, users can leverage their existing mental models and focus on their actual tasks rather than learning your unique interface. Violating conventions without strong justification forces users to relearn basic interactions, creating unnecessary friction.

## Key Takeaways

- Users transfer expectations built from familiar products to new, similar-appearing ones.
- Leveraging existing mental models enables superior user experiences where users can focus on their tasks rather than learning new interfaces.
- When implementing design changes, reduce friction by allowing users to continue with familiar versions temporarily while they transition to new patterns.

## Origins

Jakob's Law was established by Jakob Nielsen, a User Advocate and principal of Nielsen Norman Group (co-founded with Dr. Donald A. Norman, former VP of research at Apple Computer). Nielsen pioneered the "discount usability engineering" movement for rapid, cost-effective UI improvements and developed several usability methodologies, including heuristic evaluation. His observation that "users spend most of their time on other websites" became a foundational principle for web and product design, emphasizing that consistency with external standards and conventions is often more important than internal design innovation.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Follow platform conventions**: Place navigation where users expect it (top or left for web, bottom for mobile). Use standard icons for common actions (hamburger for menu, magnifying glass for search, gear for settings).
- **Don't innovate on basic interactions**: Scroll behavior, form interactions, link styling, button patterns — these should match what users know from other products in your category.
- **Study competitors and category leaders**: Understanding what patterns are standard in your product category helps identify which conventions to follow and which genuine opportunities for differentiation exist.
- **Innovate where it matters**: Jakob's Law doesn't mean never innovate — it means innovate in your value proposition and unique features, not in basic navigation and interaction patterns.
- **Manage transitions carefully**: When redesigning, provide transition periods where users can access the old interface while learning the new one. Gradual evolution is less disruptive than revolutionary redesigns.
- **Violated conventions need strong justification**: Every departure from convention must be earned by providing measurably superior usability. "Different" is not "better."

## Further Reading Insights

**Nielsen Norman Group (Power Law of Learning)** — The power law of learning explains why established interfaces persist: users have already practiced familiar patterns thousands of times, reaching performance plateaus. New designs start from scratch on the learning curve, requiring significant effort before potential benefits materialize. Before introducing non-standard design, ask: will it deliver substantially better results once mastered? Will users persist until proficiency? Can learning be accelerated through frequent exposure? Consistency isn't stagnation — it's respecting cognitive constraints. Innovation requires justification, not just novelty.

**Nielsen Norman Group (Top 10 Web Design Mistakes)** — Nielsen identified "violating design conventions" as a critical web design mistake, noting that users spend most of their time on other websites and expect your site to match common practices. Other related mistakes include not changing visited link colors (users rely on this to track their browsing), non-scannable text, and opening new windows that disable the back button — all violations of users' learned expectations from other sites.

**Jon Yablonski (Familiar vs Novel)** — The tension between familiarity and novelty in design requires careful navigation. Familiar patterns reduce cognitive load but can feel stale; novel approaches create excitement but add learning cost. The most successful designs find the "sweet spot" — novel enough to feel fresh and differentiated, familiar enough that users can immediately accomplish their goals.
