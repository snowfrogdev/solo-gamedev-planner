# Occam's Razor

Among competing hypotheses that predict equally well, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected.

## Description

Occam's Razor (also spelled Ockham's Razor) is a problem-solving principle that favors simplicity: when multiple solutions exist, the simplest one — requiring the fewest assumptions, the fewest elements, the least complexity — is usually the best choice. In design, this translates to a relentless focus on removing unnecessary elements, reducing complexity, and ensuring every component serves a clear purpose. The principle doesn't advocate for oversimplification, but for purposeful simplicity — keeping everything as simple as possible, but no simpler.

## Key Takeaways

- The best method for reducing complexity is to avoid it in the first place.
- Analyze each element and remove as many as possible, without compromising the overall function.
- Consider completion only when no additional items can be removed.
- Simple solutions are more sustainable — complex systems break down with more moving parts.

## Origins

Occam's Razor (Latin: lex parsimoniae, "law of parsimony") is attributed to William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347), an English Franciscan friar, scholastic philosopher, and theologian. The concept actually predates Ockham — Aristotle expressed similar ideas, and Robert Grosseteste emphasized that solutions "which need the fewer premises" are superior "other circumstances being equal." Ockham popularized the principle, which became a cornerstone of scientific methodology and rational thought. Albert Einstein captured the philosophy elegantly: "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler."

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Audit every element**: For each visual element, interaction, and piece of content, ask: does this serve a clear purpose for the user? If not, remove it.
- **Reduce before adding**: When solving a design problem, first try removing elements rather than adding new ones. Often the simplest solution is subtracting complexity.
- **Avoid feature creep**: Resist the temptation to add features "just in case." Every feature adds complexity, cognitive load, and maintenance burden. Build what users need, not what might be nice to have.
- **Simplify workflows**: Look for steps in user flows that can be eliminated, combined, or automated. Every step is an opportunity for the user to abandon the task.
- **Clear visual hierarchy**: A simple, clear visual hierarchy communicates structure more effectively than elaborate visual treatments. Let content and functionality speak for themselves.
- **Beware of complexity bias**: People often perceive complex solutions as more thorough or sophisticated, even when simpler solutions are equally or more effective.

## Further Reading Insights

**Farnam Street** — Occam's Razor traces back to Aristotle and was developed by William of Ockham. The simplest explanation isn't automatically correct — it requires empirical evidence and remains vulnerable to confirmation bias. Lisa Randall noted that "the world is more complicated than any of us would have conceived." The tool works best for initial conclusions before complete information emerges, requiring ongoing refinement. Use alongside other mental models, not as a replacement for rigorous analysis.

**Farnam Street (Complexity Bias)** — Complexity bias is our tendency to perceive simple things as unnecessarily complicated. When faced with competing explanations, we gravitate toward more complex ones even when simpler answers are more accurate. Companies exploit this by using confusing terminology that sounds scientifically superior. Einstein and Steve Jobs excelled at "sifting the essential from the non-essential." The antidote: apply Occam's Razor deliberately, choosing the hypothesis requiring the fewest assumptions.

**Interaction Design Foundation (Mads Soegaard)** — In design, Occam's Razor means the simplest solution is always the best — as long as it solves the problem. This doesn't mean minimalism for its own sake; it means purposeful simplicity. Remove unnecessary elements, but ensure that what remains is sufficient for users to accomplish their goals effectively.

**Jon Yablonski** — Designing with Occam's Razor means starting from the assumption that every element must earn its place. Challenge each visual element, interaction, and piece of copy. If removing it doesn't reduce the effectiveness of the design, it shouldn't be there.
