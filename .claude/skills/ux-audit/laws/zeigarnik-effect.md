# Zeigarnik Effect

People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed tasks.

## Description

The Zeigarnik Effect describes how incomplete tasks create a state of cognitive tension that keeps them at the forefront of the mind. The brain treats unfinished work as an "open loop" that demands resolution, making incomplete tasks more memorable and attention-grabbing than completed ones. In interface design, this principle can be leveraged to encourage task completion, maintain engagement through multi-step flows, and provide appropriate indicators that help users return to unfinished work.

## Key Takeaways

- Invite content discovery by providing clear signifiers of additional content beyond the visible area.
- Providing artificial progress towards a goal will help ensure users are more likely to have the motivation to complete that task.
- Provide a clear indication of progress in order to motivate users to complete tasks.
- Incomplete tasks create cognitive tension that keeps users engaged and motivated to finish.

## Origins

Bluma Wulfovna Zeigarnik (1900-1988) was a Soviet psychologist and psychiatrist affiliated with the Berlin School of experimental psychology and the Vygotsky Circle. The effect was reportedly inspired by an observation in a restaurant: a waiter could remember complex unpaid orders in perfect detail but immediately forgot them once the bill was settled. During the 1920s, Zeigarnik conducted controlled research comparing memory retention between incomplete and complete tasks, discovering that incomplete tasks are easier to remember than successful ones. This finding became known as the Zeigarnik Effect. She later worked at the Institute of Higher Nervous Activity and founded the Department of Psychology. In 1983, Zeigarnik received the Lewin Memorial Award for her contributions to psychology.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Progress indicators in multi-step flows**: Show users how many steps they've completed and how many remain. The visible incompleteness creates motivation to continue.
- **Profile completion bars**: Services like LinkedIn use completion indicators to motivate users to fill in additional profile information. The visible "gap" between current and complete status drives engagement.
- **Save draft functionality**: Allow users to save incomplete work and return to it later. The cognitive tension of the unfinished task will help bring them back.
- **Peek content beyond the fold**: Show partial content below the visible area to signal that more exists, motivating users to scroll.
- **Endowed progress effect**: Start progress indicators at a non-zero point (e.g., "Step 1 of 5 complete" when they arrive) to leverage both the Zeigarnik Effect and the Goal-Gradient Effect together.
- **Notification badges for incomplete items**: Show counts of unread messages, incomplete tasks, or pending actions to leverage the cognitive tension of incompleteness.
- **Use ethically**: The Zeigarnik Effect can be exploited to create anxiety and compulsive behavior (endless notifications, artificial incompleteness). Use it to genuinely help users accomplish their goals, not to manipulate engagement.

## Further Reading Insights

**Nielsen Norman Group (Feifei Liu)** — The Zeigarnik Effect in UX design means unfinished tasks are more memorable than completed ones. The psychological principle creates cognitive tension that keeps unfinished work at the forefront of users' minds. Implementation should be balanced — overly artificial or manipulative use of incompletion can frustrate rather than engage. The goal is to guide genuine task completion, not exploit user psychology unethically.

**UX Collective (Canvs Editorial)** — The endowed progress effect (giving users a head start) combines with the Zeigarnik Effect: when users see they've already made progress, the incomplete status becomes more motivating because they're both closer to the goal and aware of the gap. Pre-filling progress bars or marking initial steps as complete increases completion rates.

**Farnam Street** — The Goal Gradient Hypothesis connects to the Zeigarnik Effect: as people approach a goal, they accelerate their efforts. The Zeigarnik Effect explains the initial motivation (the tension of incompleteness) while the goal gradient explains the acceleration (increasing effort near completion). Together, they form a powerful framework for designing engaging multi-step experiences.

**Abhishek Chakraborty (Medium)** — The Zeigarnik Effect explains why cliffhangers work in entertainment and why incomplete tasks haunt our to-do lists. In design psychology, the principle should be applied thoughtfully: create clear indicators of incompleteness that help users track their progress, not artificial incompleteness designed to create anxiety.
