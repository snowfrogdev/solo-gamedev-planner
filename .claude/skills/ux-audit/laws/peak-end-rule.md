# Peak-End Rule

People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its most intense point and at its end, rather than based on the sum or average of every moment of the experience.

## Description

The Peak-End Rule describes a cognitive bias in which people evaluate experiences based on two key moments: the most emotionally intense point (the "peak") and the final moment (the "end"). The overall duration of the experience has surprisingly little effect on the memory — a phenomenon called "duration neglect." This means that a long, mostly mediocre experience with one great moment and a satisfying ending will be remembered more positively than a consistently good but unremarkable experience. For design, this implies that investing in peak moments and endings yields disproportionate returns on user satisfaction.

## Key Takeaways

- Focus design efforts on the most emotionally intense points and concluding moments of user interactions.
- Identify and enhance moments when your product delivers maximum helpfulness, value, or enjoyment.
- Recognize that negative experiences leave stronger impressions in memory than positive ones.
- The duration of an experience matters less than the quality of its peak and ending.

## Origins

The Peak-End Rule was established through a landmark 1993 study by Daniel Kahneman, Barbara Fredrickson, Charles Schreiber, and Donald Redelmeier titled "When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End." Researchers had participants submerge their hands in 14°C water — one group for 60 seconds, another for 90 seconds with the final 30 seconds at slightly warmer 15°C. Despite the longer total discomfort, participants preferred repeating the second trial, demonstrating that the ending significantly influenced their memory of the experience. Kahneman's broader research on the distinction between the "experiencing self" and the "remembering self" provided the theoretical framework for understanding why memories diverge from moment-to-moment experiences.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Design peak moments deliberately**: Identify where your product can create moments of delight — task completion celebrations, helpful suggestions at the right moment, or unexpectedly smooth interactions.
- **Optimize endings**: Final screens in key flows (order confirmations, onboarding completions, export successes) deserve special design attention. Make them feel rewarding and conclusive.
- **Handle errors as peak moments**: Error states and failures are often the most emotionally intense moments in an experience — and negative peaks are remembered even more strongly than positive ones. Invest heavily in helpful, empathetic error messaging.
- **Avoid frustrating endings**: Don't end experiences with aggressive exit surveys, "don't leave!" popups, or confusing confirmation screens. These create negative endings that taint the entire experience in memory.
- **Map emotional journeys**: Chart the emotional arc of key user flows. Identify where peaks (positive and negative) occur and where the experience ends. Invest design effort at those specific points.
- **Progress indicators that accelerate**: Progress bars that speed up toward the end create a more satisfying conclusion than linear progress, leveraging the peak-end rule to improve perceived experience.

## Further Reading Insights

**Nielsen Norman Group (Lexie Kane)** — The peak-end rule means designers should amplify positive moments through visual celebrations, confirmatory animations, and reinforcement of accomplishments. Negative peaks must be mitigated: ensure reliable navigation, clear text, and reduced intrusive interruptions. For endings, design graceful conclusions and avoid aggressive retention attempts. Error states are often the most memorable moments — explain what went wrong clearly, provide realistic timeframes, and never blame users.

**Kahneman et al. (1993)** — The original cold-water experiment demonstrated that people choose experiences with better endings even when those experiences involve more total discomfort. This finding revealed the fundamental distinction between the experiencing self (which lives moment-to-moment) and the remembering self (which evaluates based on peaks and endings).

**Jennifer Clinehens** — Uber uses psychology including the peak-end rule to perfect their customer experience. The app design creates positive peaks (seeing the car approach on the map) and positive endings (seamless payment that eliminates fumbling for cash or calculating tips). These designed moments create memories that are more positive than the full experience might warrant.
