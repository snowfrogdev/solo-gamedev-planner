# Miller's Law

The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.

## Description

Miller's Law describes the cognitive limitation of human working memory, which can typically hold approximately 5-9 discrete items at once. This constraint has profound implications for information design: when users are presented with more information than working memory can handle, they become overwhelmed, make more errors, and process information less efficiently. The solution is chunking — organizing information into meaningful groups that each count as a single "item" in working memory, effectively increasing the amount of information that can be processed at once.

## Key Takeaways

- Don't use the "magical number seven" to justify unnecessary design limitations — the number is approximate and varies by individual and context.
- Organize content into smaller chunks to help users process, understand, and memorize information more easily.
- Short-term memory capacity varies per individual based on prior knowledge and situational context.
- Chunking is the primary technique for working within memory constraints — group related items into meaningful units.

## Origins

In 1956, cognitive psychologist George A. Miller published "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two: Some Limits on Our Capacity for Processing Information," one of the most cited papers in psychology. Miller established that the span of immediate memory and absolute judgment were both limited to around 7 pieces of information. The concept centers on "bits" as the basic unit of information — the data needed to choose between two equally probable alternatives. Miller's key insight was about chunking: while working memory holds ~7 items, each "item" can be a chunk of related information, dramatically increasing effective capacity through organization.

Later research by Nelson Cowan (2001, 2010) refined Miller's estimate, suggesting the true capacity of working memory is closer to 4 chunks for most people, with the original higher estimates inflated by rehearsal strategies and long-term memory assistance.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Chunk information visually**: Break long lists, large data sets, and complex forms into groups of 3-5 related items. Phone numbers (555-867-5309) and credit card numbers (4532 1234 5678 9012) are classic chunking examples.
- **Limit visible options**: Navigation menus, feature lists, and dashboard displays should group items into meaningful categories rather than presenting flat lists of many items.
- **Progressive disclosure**: Don't present all information at once. Show the most important items first and let users explore deeper levels on demand.
- **Don't take the number literally**: Miller's Law doesn't mean every menu should have exactly 7 items. The principle is about cognitive load management through chunking, not a specific number to target.
- **Consider context**: Users with domain expertise can process more items in their specialty area because they naturally chunk related concepts. Novice users need more aggressive chunking.
- **Visual grouping supports chunking**: Use the Gestalt principles (proximity, common region, similarity) to visually reinforce the chunking structure of your information.

## Further Reading Insights

**Nelson Cowan (NCBI)** — "The Magical Mystery Four: How is Working Memory Capacity Limited, and Why?" argues that the true limit of working memory is closer to 3-5 chunks, not 7. Miller's original estimate was inflated by participants using rehearsal strategies. The practical implication: design for 4 chunks of information rather than 7 when the information is unfamiliar to users and rehearsal is unlikely.

**Jon Yablonski (Prototypr)** — Design principles for reducing cognitive load extend Miller's Law into practical techniques: limit the number of choices, use progressive disclosure, group related information visually, and leverage familiar patterns. The goal is not to hit a specific number but to minimize the cognitive effort required to process and act on information.

**Khan Academy** — Miller's Law and chunking explain why some interfaces feel intuitive while others feel overwhelming. The key is not the raw number of elements but how they're organized. Well-chunked information (addresses broken into street/city/state/zip) requires less working memory than the same information presented as an undifferentiated stream.
