# Law of Uniform Connectedness

Elements that are visually connected are perceived as more related than elements with no connection.

## Description

The Law of Uniform Connectedness is a Gestalt principle stating that elements linked by visual connectors — lines, arrows, color bands, or other connecting elements — are perceived as more strongly related than elements that merely share proximity, similarity, or common region. Visual connections create explicit relationships that override implicit grouping cues, making this principle one of the most powerful tools for communicating element relationships in complex interfaces.

## Key Takeaways

- Group similar functions using visual connections like colors, lines, frames, or shapes.
- Use tangible connecting references (lines, arrows, etc.) between elements to establish visual relationships.
- Apply uniform connectedness to demonstrate context and emphasize relationships among comparable items.
- Visual connections create stronger perceived relationships than proximity alone.

## Origins

The grouping principles derive from Gestalt psychology, initially proposed by Gestalt psychologists to explain how humans naturally perceive objects as organized patterns — a concept called Prägnanz. These psychologists theorized that the mind possesses an inherent tendency to recognize patterns based on specific rules. The Law of Uniform Connectedness was formally described by Stephen Palmer and Irvin Rock in 1994 as an additional Gestalt principle beyond the classical five (Proximity, Similarity, Continuity, Closure, and Common Fate). Palmer and Rock demonstrated that visual connections between elements create grouping effects that can override other Gestalt principles.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Progress indicators and steppers**: Connect steps in a multi-step process with lines or progress bars to show the sequential relationship and current position.
- **Flowcharts and decision trees**: Use connecting lines and arrows to show how elements relate to each other in complex workflows or decision processes.
- **Breadcrumb navigation**: Use separators (arrows, chevrons, slashes) to show the hierarchical connection between navigation levels.
- **Timeline designs**: Connect events chronologically with lines or visual pathways to show progression and relationships between events.
- **Data visualization**: Use connecting lines in charts to show trends, relationships between data points, or hierarchical relationships in tree diagrams.
- **Form field connections**: Visually connect related form fields (like "Start Date" and "End Date") with lines or shared visual treatments to communicate their relationship.
- **Tab and content connections**: Visually connect active tab labels to their content panels through shared borders, colors, or positioning to clarify which content belongs to which tab.

## Further Reading Insights

**Smashing Magazine (Steven Bradley)** — Uniform connectedness provides explicit visual grouping that requires no interpretation. Visual lines or connectors between elements create stronger perceived relationships than proximity alone. Designers should use connecting lines in flowcharts, breadcrumb trails in navigation, and linking visual elements in infographics. The principle is especially valuable in complex interfaces where proximity alone is insufficient to communicate relationships.

**Interaction Design Foundation (Mads Soegaard)** — The laws of proximity, uniform connectedness, and continuation work together to create visual flow. Uniform connectedness is the most explicit of these — while proximity suggests relationships and continuation implies them, connectedness states them directly through visual links. This makes it the most reliable grouping principle for critical relationship communication.

**Andy Rutledge** — Gestalt principles of perception, including uniform connectedness, provide the perceptual framework that all visual design operates within. Understanding these principles allows designers to create interfaces that communicate structure and relationships at the preconscious level, before users engage in deliberate analysis.
