# Serial Position Effect

Users have a propensity to best remember the first and last items in a series.

## Description

The Serial Position Effect describes how the position of an item in a sequence affects recall accuracy. Items at the beginning of a list (primacy effect) and at the end of a list (recency effect) are remembered significantly better than items in the middle. This cognitive bias has direct implications for how information, navigation items, and actions should be ordered in user interfaces — critical items should occupy the first and last positions, while less important items can be placed in the middle.

## Key Takeaways

- Placing the least important items in the middle of lists can be helpful because these items tend to be stored less frequently in long-term and working memory.
- Positioning key actions on the far left and right within elements such as navigation can increase memorization.
- The primacy effect (remembering first items) relates to long-term memory encoding; the recency effect (remembering last items) relates to short-term memory retention.

## Origins

The serial position effect was first described by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s through his pioneering research on memory. Ebbinghaus discovered that when people are asked to recall items from a list, they consistently remember the first few items (primacy effect) and the last few items (recency effect) better than items in the middle. The primacy effect occurs because early items receive more rehearsal and are more likely to be transferred to long-term memory. The recency effect occurs because the last items are still in short-term memory when recall is attempted. Successful companies like Apple, Electronic Arts, and Nike leverage this effect in their navigation and product design.

## Practical Implications for UI Design

- **Place critical navigation items at extremes**: In horizontal navigation bars, the most important items should be at the far left and far right. Less critical items go in the middle.
- **Mobile tab bars**: Bottom navigation in mobile apps should place the most important actions at the leftmost and rightmost positions.
- **List ordering**: In any list — features, menu items, product options — put the most important items first and last.
- **Pricing tables**: Place the recommended plan first or last (not in the middle) to maximize recall and selection.
- **Content strategy**: In long-form content, place the most critical information at the beginning and end. Summaries, key takeaways, and calls-to-action are most effective at these positions.
- **Form field ordering**: Place the most important or memorable form fields at the beginning and end of a form section.
- **Carousel design**: If using carousels, the first and last items will be remembered best. Place key content accordingly.

## Further Reading Insights

**Interaction Design Foundation (Euphemia Wong)** — The serial position effect provides a clear framework for prioritizing information in interface design. Designers should leverage both the primacy effect (first items are remembered through long-term memory encoding) and the recency effect (last items are retained in short-term memory). Navigation design, content ordering, and feature prioritization all benefit from placing critical items at the extremes of sequences.

**Abhishek Chakraborty (Medium)** — The serial position effect explains why certain brand names, product features, and navigation items stick in memory while others fade. In design psychology, this principle should inform the deliberate ordering of every sequence users encounter — from navigation menus to feature lists to onboarding steps.

**Andri Budzinskiy (Prototypr)** — Psychology in design provides a framework for understanding why certain layouts and orderings feel more intuitive than others. The serial position effect, combined with other cognitive principles, helps designers create interfaces that work with human memory rather than against it.
